# Cover Letter

**To:** Editor-in-Chief, *Computers in Biology and Medicine*

**Date:** March 20, 2026

**Re:** Submission of manuscript entitled "Transformer, CNN, or Both? A Systematic Benchmark for ECG Arrhythmia Classification Across Architectures, Datasets, and Deployment Constraints"

---

Dear Editor,

We are pleased to submit our manuscript for consideration for publication in *Computers in Biology and Medicine*. This work addresses a critical and timely gap in the ECG deep learning literature: the absence of a controlled, head-to-head benchmark comparing CNN, Transformer, and hybrid architectures under unified experimental conditions.

**Why this work matters:** Despite the rapid proliferation of deep learning architectures for ECG arrhythmia classification, cross-study comparisons remain unreliable due to inconsistent preprocessing pipelines, dataset splits, and evaluation metrics. Our systematic benchmark evaluates six representative architectures across three public datasets (MIT-BIH, PTB-XL, CPSC2018) under strictly identical conditions, providing the first controlled evidence for architecture selection in clinical deployment.

**Key findings:**

1. Hybrid CNN-Transformer architectures achieve the highest classification performance (macro-F1: 0.946 on MIT-BIH, 0.901 on PTB-XL, 0.867 on CPSC2018), significantly outperforming pure CNN and pure Transformer baselines (McNemar's test, p < 0.05).

2. Pure CNN models retain a 3–5× computational efficiency advantage, making them preferable for edge and wearable deployment.

3. A Pareto frontier analysis yields practical architecture selection guidelines mapping deployment constraints to optimal model families.

**Fit with journal scope:** This manuscript directly aligns with the journal's focus on computational methods in biomedicine, particularly biomedical signal processing and deep learning for clinical decision support. The evaluation framework and publicly released code will benefit the growing community of researchers working on ECG analysis.
